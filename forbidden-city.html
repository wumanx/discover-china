Overview

The Forbidden City, known in Chinese as Zijincheng (紫禁城), stands at the heart of Beijing as the world's most magnificent imperial palace complex. Built between 1406 and 1420 during the Ming Dynasty, it served as the political and ceremonial center of Chinese government for nearly 500 years. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and houses the Palace Museum, welcoming over 19 million visitors annually.

The name "Forbidden City" derives from the fact that ordinary citizens were barred from entering the palace without permission — and those who trespassed faced severe punishment. Only the emperor, his family, concubines, and authorized officials could pass through its gates.

🇯🇵 Did You Know?

Japanese architect Junzō Sakakura once called the Forbidden City "the greatest architectural achievement in East Asia." The layout follows the same cosmic principles of feng shui that influenced Japanese castle and temple design, making it a familiar yet awe-inspiring experience for Japanese visitors.

Must-See Highlights

Meridian Gate (Wumen)

The imposing main entrance, where the emperor would review troops and announce the calendar. Its U-shaped design creates a grand courtyard that could hold thousands of officials during ceremonies.

Hall of Supreme Harmony

The largest wooden structure in China, rising 35 meters high. This is where the emperor held grand ceremonies — coronations, imperial weddings, and New Year celebrations. The golden throne inside is an iconic symbol of imperial power.

Hall of Central Harmony

The emperor's private rehearsal space before major ceremonies. A smaller, more intimate hall where the ruler would compose his thoughts before appearing before his court.

Hall of Preserving Harmony

Behind this hall lies the largest stone carving in China — a 250-ton ramp carved from a single block of stone, featuring dragons and clouds. It was transported to the palace by sliding it on ice during winter.

Imperial Garden

A serene retreat at the northern end of the palace, featuring ancient cypress trees, rockeries, and the Pavilion of Imperial Peace. A perfect spot to rest after exploring the vast complex.

Clock Exhibition Hall

An extraordinary collection of over 200 clocks and timepieces gifted to Qing emperors by European nations. Many are mechanical marvels that still function, including clocks shaped as automata, globes, and miniature palaces.

Practical Information

Opening Hours

  • April – October: 8:30 – 17:00 (last entry 16:10)
  • November – March: 8:30 – 16:30 (last entry 15:40)
  • Closed: Mondays (except national holidays)

Tickets

  • Peak season (Apr–Oct): ¥60
  • Off-peak (Nov–Mar): ¥40
  • Clock Exhibition: ¥10 additional
  • Treasure Gallery: ¥10 additional

Getting There

Take Subway Line 1 to Tiananmen East or Tiananmen West station. The entrance is through the Meridian Gate on the south side. Allow 3–4 hours for a full visit.

Tips for Japanese Visitors

  • Tickets must be purchased online in advance — same-day tickets are not available at the gate
  • Audio guides are available in Japanese (¥40)
  • Wear comfortable shoes — the complex is enormous
  • The north-south axis is 961 meters long; pace yourself
  • Visit on weekday mornings for fewer crowds
