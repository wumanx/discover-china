Essential Guide

Travel Tips for Japan

Everything you need to know before your China adventure — visa, transport, money, and more

Visa Information

Great News: 15-Day Visa-Free Entry!

Japanese citizens can enter China visa-free for up to 15 days for tourism, business, or visiting family. Simply present your valid Japanese passport at immigration. For stays longer than 15 days, a tourist visa (L Visa) is required, which is straightforward to obtain from the Chinese Embassy or Consulate in Japan.

  • Passport validity: Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned stay
  • L Visa (for longer stays): Single entry ¥3,000, processing time 4 business days
  • Required documents: Passport, application form, photo, hotel booking, return flight ticket
  • 144-hour transit visa exemption: Available in Shanghai, Beijing, and other cities for transiting to a third country

Flights from Japan

Tokyo → Beijing

3.5 hours. Multiple daily flights from Narita and Haneda. From ¥25,000 round trip (budget airlines).

Tokyo → Shanghai

3 hours. Most frequent route. From ¥20,000 round trip. Spring Airlines offers great deals.

Osaka → Shanghai

2.5 hours. Peach Aviation and Spring Airlines have budget options from ¥15,000 round trip.

Direct to Chengdu/Xi'an

4–5 hours. Sichuan Airlines and other carriers. From ¥30,000 round trip.

Money & Payments

Currency

Chinese Yuan (CNY, ¥). As of 2026, approximately 1 JPY ≈ 0.048 CNY (or 100 JPY ≈ 4.8 CNY). Exchange rates are stable.

Payment Methods

  • Alipay & WeChat Pay: The dominant payment methods in China. Both now accept foreign credit cards — download and set up before your trip. You can pay for almost everything: restaurants, taxis, street food, tickets
  • Credit Cards: Visa and Mastercard accepted at hotels and large stores, but not at smaller shops
  • Cash: Still useful for small vendors, but increasingly less necessary. Exchange at airports or banks
  • ATMs: Available at airports and major banks. Look for UnionPay logos

💡 Pro Tip: Set Up Mobile Payments

Download Alipay and WeChat before arriving in Japan. Both apps allow foreign tourists to link international credit cards. This is essential — many small restaurants and shops in China are cashless. Without mobile payments, you may struggle with everyday purchases.

Getting Around China

High-Speed Rail

China's high-speed rail network is the world's largest and most impressive. Trains reach 350 km/h, connecting all major cities comfortably and efficiently.

  • Beijing → Shanghai: 4.5 hours (¥553 second class)
  • Beijing → Xi'an: 4.5 hours (¥515 second class)
  • Shanghai → Hangzhou: 1 hour (¥73 second class)
  • Shanghai → Chengdu: 12 hours (sleeper train recommended, ¥590)
  • Book via: Trip.com (English interface) or 12306.cn (official, more complex)

City Transportation

  • Metro: All major cities have modern subway systems. Get a transit card or use Alipay
  • Taxi: Use DiDi (China's Uber) — available in Alipay app. Very affordable
  • Bus: Extensive networks but can be confusing without Chinese language skills

Internet & Communication

  • VPN: Google, LINE, Instagram, and other services are blocked. Install a reliable VPN before arriving in China
  • Wi-Fi: Available in hotels, cafes, and most public spaces
  • SIM card: Buy a China Unicom or China Mobile SIM at the airport for data access. eSIM options also available
  • Translation apps: Download Baidu Translate or Google Translate with offline Chinese pack
  • WeChat: Essential for communication — most Chinese people use it exclusively

Health & Safety

  • Food safety: Generally good in cities. Drink bottled water. Street food is usually safe if cooked fresh
  • Medical: International clinics available in major cities. Travel insurance recommended
  • Safety: China is one of the safest countries for tourists. Violent crime is extremely rare
  • Air quality: Varies by city and season. Check AQI readings; masks are readily available
  • Vaccinations: No special vaccinations required for Japan-to-China travel

Useful Apps

📱 Alipay

Payment, DiDi taxi, attraction tickets, metro codes — your all-in-one China app

📱 WeChat

Messaging, payments, mini-programs for services. Every Chinese person uses it

📱 Trip.com

Book trains, flights, hotels in English. Essential for high-speed rail tickets

📱 Baidu Maps

Best map app for China (Google Maps has inaccuracies). Available in Japanese

Etiquette Quick Guide

  • Greetings: A nod or slight bow is appropriate. Handshakes are also common in business settings
  • Tipping: Not expected and sometimes refused. Don't tip at restaurants
  • Chopsticks: Never stick chopsticks upright in rice (it resembles funeral incense)
  • Photography: Always ask before photographing people. Some sites prohibit flash
  • Shoes: Remove shoes when entering someone's home (similar to Japan)
  • Loud conversation: Speaking on the phone in public transport is considered impolite
